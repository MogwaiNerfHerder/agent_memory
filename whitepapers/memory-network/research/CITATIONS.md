# Memory Network Whitepaper — Citations

Compiled 2026-05-14. Each claim lists 1–3 authoritative sources with URL, year, and a quotable line. Notes call out which source is strongest and any caveats about over-quoted statistics.

## Claim 1: Knowledge workers spend a significant fraction of the workweek searching for information they cannot find.

- **McKinsey Global Institute, "The social economy: Unlocking value and productivity through social technologies," 2012** — "The average interaction worker spends an estimated 28 percent of the workweek managing e-mail and nearly 20 percent looking for information." — https://www.mckinsey.com/industries/technology-media-and-telecommunications/our-insights/the-social-economy
- **IDC, "The Hidden Costs of Information Work" (Susan Feldman), 2005 / updated 2012** — "An enterprise employing 1,000 knowledge workers wastes $48,000 per week, or nearly $2.5 million per year, due to an inability to locate and retrieve information." — https://computhink.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/10/IDC20on20The20High20Cost20Of20Not20Finding20Information.pdf
- **Panopto / YouGov, "Workplace Knowledge and Productivity Report," 2018** — "U.S. knowledge workers waste 5.3 hours every week either waiting for vital information from their colleagues or working to recreate existing institutional knowledge." — https://www.panopto.com/company/news/inefficient-knowledge-sharing-costs-large-businesses-47-million-per-year/
- **Forrester Total Economic Impact of Glean (commissioned), Sept 2024** — "Each user that adopts Glean's genAI capability saves an additional 50 hours per year" (composite 10,000-employee org). — https://tei.forrester.com/go/Glean/workAIplatform/
- *Notes:* The McKinsey "20%" is the canonical, most-cited figure but is now 14 years old. Pair it with the Panopto 2018 number (5.3 hrs/week ≈ 13% of a 40-hour week) for a more recent corroboration. The IDC dollar figure is famous but is from a vendor-commissioned white paper — cite carefully. The Forrester/Glean number is vendor-commissioned and should be flagged as such.

## Claim 2: It takes ~23 minutes to recover focus after an interruption.

- **Mark, G., Gonzalez, V. & Harris, J., "No Task Left Behind? Examining the Nature of Fragmented Work," CHI 2005** — "Most interrupted work was resumed on the same day — 81.9% — and was resumed, on average, in 23 minutes and 15 seconds." — https://ics.uci.edu/~gmark/CHI2005.pdf
- **Mark, G., Gudith, D. & Klocke, U., "The Cost of Interrupted Work: More Speed and Stress," CHI 2008** — Lab study showing interrupted workers compensate by working faster, but "experience more stress, higher frustration, time pressure and effort." — https://ics.uci.edu/~gmark/chi08-mark.pdf
- **Microsoft Work Trend Index, 2024 (Microsoft + LinkedIn)** — "Employees are interrupted every two minutes" by meetings, emails, and pings during core work hours. — https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/worklab/work-trend-index and https://news.microsoft.com/annual-wti-2024/
- *Notes:* IMPORTANT NUANCE — the "23 minutes 15 seconds" figure is from the **2005** "No Task Left Behind" paper, not the 2008 "Cost of Interrupted Work" paper that is most commonly miscited. The 23-min figure measures *time until the original task is resumed* (with ~2 intervening tasks in between), NOT "time to fully recover deep focus" — that interpretation is a popular embellishment not supported by the primary data. Cite the 2005 paper directly and describe the metric accurately. Microsoft 2024 is the freshest corroboration of the broader fragmentation thesis.

## Claim 3: Enterprise AI/genAI pilots fail at high rates (60–95%).

- **MIT NANDA / MIT Media Lab, "The GenAI Divide: State of AI in Business 2025," 2025** — "About 95% of organizations are getting zero return" on their generative AI pilots; only ~5% achieve rapid revenue acceleration. (Based on 150 leader interviews, 350-employee survey, and 300 public deployments.) — https://fortune.com/2025/08/18/mit-report-95-percent-generative-ai-pilots-at-companies-failing-cfo/
- **RAND Corporation, "The Root Causes of Failure for Artificial Intelligence Projects" (RR-A2680-1), 2024** — "By some estimates, more than 80 percent of AI projects fail — twice the rate of failure for IT projects that do not involve AI." — https://www.rand.org/pubs/research_reports/RRA2680-1.html
- **Gartner press release, "Gartner Predicts 30% of Generative AI Projects Will Be Abandoned After Proof of Concept by End of 2025," July 2024** — "At least 30% of generative AI projects will be abandoned after proof of concept by the end of 2025, due to poor data quality, inadequate risk controls, escalating costs, or unclear business value." — https://www.gartner.com/en/newsroom/press-releases/2024-07-29-gartner-predicts-30-percent-of-generative-ai-projects-will-be-abandoned-after-proof-of-concept-by-end-of-2025
- **Gartner press release, "Lack of AI-Ready Data Puts AI Projects at Risk," Feb 2025** — "Through 2026, organizations will abandon 60% of AI projects unsupported by AI-ready data." — https://www.gartner.com/en/newsroom/press-releases/2025-02-26-lack-of-ai-ready-data-puts-ai-projects-at-risk
- **BCG, "AI Adoption in 2024: 74% of Companies Struggle to Achieve and Scale Value," Oct 2024** — "Only 26% of companies have developed the necessary set of capabilities to move beyond proofs of concept and generate tangible value… only 4% are creating substantial value." — https://www.bcg.com/press/24october2024-ai-adoption-in-2024-74-of-companies-struggle-to-achieve-and-scale-value
- *Notes:* The MIT 95% number is the strongest single sound bite right now and got massive press in 2025. The RAND 80% is the most defensible academic-style citation. Gartner's 30%/60% predictions are the right authority for executive audiences. Use MIT + RAND as your one-two punch; cite Gartner for "abandoned-after-PoC" framing; cite BCG for the value-realization angle.

## Claim 4: Tribal knowledge loss when employees leave — turnover costs and the "knowledge walks out the door" effect.

- **SHRM (Society for Human Resource Management), "The Myth of Replaceability: Preparing for the Loss of Key Employees"** — Replacing an employee costs roughly "50% to 200% of their annual salary," depending on level and specialization; institutional knowledge "walks out the door." — https://www.shrm.org/executive-network/insights/myth-replaceability-preparing-loss-key-employees
- **Panopto / YouGov, "Workplace Knowledge and Productivity Report," 2018** — "42 percent of institutional knowledge is unique to the individual" who holds it; "81% of employees state that knowledge gained from hands-on experience is the hardest to replace once it's lost." — https://www.panopto.com/company/news/inefficient-knowledge-sharing-costs-large-businesses-47-million-per-year/
- **Panopto Workplace Knowledge Report, 2018** — "The average large US business loses $47 million in productivity each year as a direct result of inefficient knowledge sharing." — https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/inefficient-knowledge-sharing-costs-large-businesses-47-million-per-year-300681971.html
- *Notes:* Panopto's "42% of institutional knowledge is unique to the individual" is the perfect "tribal knowledge walks out the door" stat — vivid and quantified. SHRM is the gold-standard citation for the cost-of-replacement range. The original Bersin/Deloitte and Center for American Progress turnover-cost studies are also widely cited if you need a third source.

## Claim 5: RAG limitations — vector search retrieves *similar text*, not *connected facts*; knowledge graphs complement RAG.

- **Microsoft Research, Edge et al., "From Local to Global: A Graph RAG Approach to Query-Focused Summarization," arXiv:2404.16130, April 2024** — "For global sensemaking questions over datasets in the 1 million token range, GraphRAG leads to substantial improvements over a conventional RAG baseline for both the comprehensiveness and diversity of generated answers." — https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.16130
- **Microsoft Research blog, "GraphRAG: Unlocking LLM discovery on narrative private data," Feb 2024** — "Baseline RAG struggles to connect the dots. This happens when answering a question requires traversing disparate pieces of information through their shared attributes…" — https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/blog/graphrag-unlocking-llm-discovery-on-narrative-private-data/
- **Han et al., "Retrieval-Augmented Generation with Graphs (GraphRAG)," arXiv:2501.00309, Jan 2025 (survey)** — Comprehensive survey arguing graph-structured retrieval addresses multi-hop reasoning failures inherent to chunk-based vector RAG. — https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.00309
- **Peng et al., "Graph Retrieval-Augmented Generation: A Survey," ACM TOIS / arXiv:2408.08921, 2024** — Peer-reviewed survey of graph-RAG techniques and benchmarks. — https://arxiv.org/abs/2408.08921
- **NVIDIA Technical Blog, "Insights, Techniques, and Evaluation for LLM-Driven Knowledge Graphs," 2024** — "Combining LLMs with knowledge graphs enhances retrieval-augmented generation… improving reasoning, accuracy, and reducing hallucinations." Reports HybridRAG and GraphRAG outperform vector-only RAG on correctness benchmarks. — https://developer.nvidia.com/blog/insights-techniques-and-evaluation-for-llm-driven-knowledge-graphs/
- **IBM, "What is GraphRAG?"** — Vendor-neutral explainer used by enterprise architects. — https://www.ibm.com/think/topics/graphrag
- *Notes:* The Microsoft "connect the dots" framing is the single best one-liner for an executive audience — that's the quote to lead with. Back it with the Peng et al. ACM survey for academic credibility, and NVIDIA for hardware/vendor endorsement. Avoid leaning on Diffbot's "3.4×" or Writer's "86% RobustQA" numbers without flagging that they are vendor-published benchmarks.

## Claim 6: Knowledge graphs in enterprise — adoption and big-tech examples.

- **Gartner Hype Cycle for Artificial Intelligence, 2024** — Knowledge graphs positioned on the **Slope of Enlightenment**, indicating maturity and growing enterprise adoption. (Direct Gartner doc paywalled; widely-cited Ontoforce summary:) — https://www.ontoforce.com/blog/knowledge-graphs-on-the-rise-gartners-2024-ai-hype-cycle-shows-their-growing-impact
- **Noy, Gao, Jain, Narayanan, Patterson & Taylor, "Industry-scale Knowledge Graphs: Lessons and Challenges," ACM Queue / Communications of the ACM, 2019** — Co-authored by knowledge graph leads at Google, Microsoft, Facebook, eBay, and IBM; describes graphs as core infrastructure: "Knowledge graphs are critical to many enterprises today." — https://queue.acm.org/detail.cfm?id=3332266 and https://cacm.acm.org/practice/industry-scale-knowledge-graphs/
- **LinkedIn Engineering — Economic Graph** — LinkedIn's Economic Graph maps "members, jobs, companies, skills, and educational institutions" across 1B+ members; used as a proof point for knowledge-graph value at scale. — https://economicgraph.linkedin.com/
- **Google, "Introducing the Knowledge Graph: things, not strings" (Singhal), May 2012** — The original announcement; foundational reference for knowledge-graph-powered search. — https://blog.google/products/search/introducing-knowledge-graph-things-not/
- *Notes:* Gartner positioning on Slope of Enlightenment is the strongest "this is mainstream now" signal for executives. The ACM Queue paper is the rare peer-reviewed account where Google + Microsoft + Facebook + eBay + IBM authors all describe their internal knowledge graphs. Use those two together. Note: I could not find a clean public Gartner-published source for an "X% of enterprises will use knowledge graphs by year Y" adoption stat — soften any specific adoption-percentage claim or attribute it directly to a vendor (Neo4j, TigerGraph) survey rather than Gartner.

## Claim 7: MCP (Model Context Protocol) momentum and adoption as de facto standard.

- **Anthropic, "Introducing the Model Context Protocol," Nov 25, 2024** — "MCP is a new standard for connecting AI assistants to the systems where data lives, including content repositories, business tools, and development environments." — https://www.anthropic.com/news/model-context-protocol
- **OpenAI / Sam Altman, March 2025** — OpenAI announced MCP support across the Agents SDK, Responses API, and ChatGPT desktop app. (Coverage:) — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Model_Context_Protocol
- **Anthropic, "Donating the Model Context Protocol and establishing the Agentic AI Foundation," Dec 2025** — MCP donated to a Linux Foundation-affiliated foundation co-founded by Anthropic, Block, and OpenAI, with Google, Microsoft, AWS, Cloudflare, and Bloomberg. — https://www.anthropic.com/news/donating-the-model-context-protocol-and-establishing-of-the-agentic-ai-foundation
- **The New Stack, "Why the Model Context Protocol Won," 2025** — Industry analysis on MCP becoming the universal connector. — https://thenewstack.io/why-the-model-context-protocol-won/
- *Notes:* The Anthropic announcement + OpenAI's adoption + the Linux Foundation donation form a clean three-beat narrative: introduced, adopted by competitor, neutrally governed. That's the strongest possible momentum story you could ask for in 13 months.

## Claim 8: "Sherpa" metaphor for AI / "AI as guide".

- **Brett Hurt (CEO, data.world), "Your Enterprise Data is a Mountain, a Context Engine is the Sherpa," Medium, 2024** — "You wouldn't embark on a high-altitude climb without a 'sherpa'. Similarly, your guide for the ascent to the summit of AI application should be a context engine, a tool that immediately opens up vast stores of your own data." — https://databrett.medium.com/your-enterprise-data-is-a-mountain-a-context-engine-is-the-sherpa-1029e101e7dc
- **Sherpa.ai (company)** — Federated-learning vendor that has used the Sherpa branding for AI assistants since 2012; demonstrates the metaphor's currency in industry naming. — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sherpa.ai
- *Notes:* The "AI Sherpa" framing is **NOT** widely used by Gartner, Forrester, McKinsey, Microsoft, or Google as an analyst category. The closest mainstream framings are Microsoft's "Copilot," Google's "AI assistant / agent," and McKinsey's "AI co-pilot." If you want to use "Sherpa" as the through-line metaphor for the whitepaper, **own it as your original framing** rather than citing it as established industry vocabulary. Hurt's piece (notably, by the CEO of a data-catalog/knowledge-graph vendor) is the strongest existing precedent and conveniently aligns with the "memory network" thesis. **Recommend softening the "industry uses this framing" angle** — say something like "while the industry has converged on 'copilot,' we propose a sharper distinction: a copilot helps you fly the plane you already know how to fly; a sherpa knows the mountain you've never climbed."
